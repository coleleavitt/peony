## (1) Self-adjusting computation and the O(affected) bound

The relevant primary sources *are* in the evidence. Acar, Blume, and Donham give a formal semantics of self-adjusting computation that "integrate[s] change propagation with the classic idea of memoization" and prove it "correct and consistent" [arXiv:1106.0478, block 1/2]. The core mechanism is the **dynamic dependence graph (DDG)**: a trace of the computation in which control and data dependencies are recorded so that, on an input change, *change propagation* re-executes only the portions of the trace whose inputs changed, reusing the rest via memoization.

The asymptotic payoff you describe — recomputation in O(affected) rather than O(total) — is exactly what the **stability theorems** in Acar's line of work establish: the cost of change propagation is bounded by the "edit distance" between the old and new execution traces, not by the size of the whole computation. The retrieved consistency-semantics paper supplies the soundness half (the propagated result equals a from-scratch recomputation); the stability half (the cost bound) comes from Acar's thesis and the PLDI/POPL papers, which the search did **not** retrieve and which you should pull directly.

For the parallel case there is a directly relevant 2021 result: Anderson, Baweja, Blelloch, and Acar, *Efficient Parallel Self-Adjusting Computation* [arXiv:2105.06712, block 1], which extends the DDG/change-propagation machinery to a work-span (parallel) cost model. This is the single most on-target paper in the evidence for your project, because it unifies items (1) and (3): it gives both the incremental *and* the parallel cost bounds in one framework.

**Mapping to linking:** the linker's job is a function from (object files, symbol tables, relocations) to (output image). If you model it as a self-adjusting computation, the stability theorem says a rebuild after editing one `.o` costs O(work touched by that `.o`'s changed symbols and relocations) plus the trace-edit overhead — *provided* you can show the dependence graph is shallow and that most outputs are stable across the edit. That "provided" is where the linker-specific invariants (item 2) do the real work.

## (2) Incremental linking and the capacity-stable invariant

The evidence returned nothing on Microsoft `/INCREMENTAL`, gold's incremental mode, or why they were abandoned — that part of your question is uncovered by the retrieved sources and I will not fabricate citations. From general knowledge (uncited): incremental linkers historically relied on **padding sections so a regrown function fits in place**, keeping every other symbol's address fixed, which avoids re-applying every absolute relocation. They were abandoned largely because (a) the invariant breaks the moment a section overflows its padding, forcing a cascading relayout; (b) modern fast from-scratch linkers (mold, lld) made the maintenance and correctness burden hard to justify; and (c) features like ICF and `--gc-sections` interact badly with address-stability.

The formal content worth extracting — and this *is* the theorem that connects to item (1) — is a **capacity-stability invariant**: define a layout as "capacity-stable under edit Δ" iff every section's post-edit size ≤ its allocated capacity. Under that invariant, you can prove that the set of relocations needing reapplication equals exactly the relocations *referencing changed symbols*, i.e., the address map is unchanged, so change propagation touches O(affected). The interesting research question is the *characterization*: for which class of edits does capacity-stability hold, and what padding policy minimizes expected cascades? No retrieved source addresses this; it is genuinely open and a good candidate for formalization.

## (3) Work-span lower bounds for parallel copy/resolution

Brent's theorem and Blelloch's greedy-scheduler bound were not retrieved (block 2 returned only real-time DAG-scheduling papers and an unrelated "Brent-McMillan algorithm" number-theory paper — none relevant). From standard theory (uncited): Brent's bound says a greedy schedule runs in T_P ≤ T_1/P + T_∞ (work/processors plus span). For **section copy**, the outputs are disjoint byte ranges, so the dependence DAG is an antichain (zero-span, embarrassingly parallel): T_∞ = O(max section size), T_1 = O(total bytes), and the greedy schedule is provably within 2× optimal. That is the easy, already-known result.

The non-trivial span comes from **symbol resolution** and **relocation patching**, which have real dependencies (a relocation can't be applied before its target's final address is known). The provably-optimal schedule for "a DAG of independent disjoint-output tasks" is precisely the greedy/list-scheduling result, and the *Efficient Parallel Self-Adjusting Computation* paper [block 1] is your bridge to making this bound hold *across incremental edits*, not just one build.

## (4) ICF as partition refinement

Hopcroft's algorithm and the ICF-as-bisimulation framing were not retrieved (block 4 found zero results plus only local-repo matches). From general knowledge (uncited): ICF folds two functions iff they are observably equivalent, which is naturally a **bisimulation / partition-refinement** problem — start with all functions in one class partitioned by opcode/relocation-target signature, then refine until stable, exactly Hopcroft's O(E log V) partition refinement over the call/reference graph. The **soundness side-condition** is the address-taken / pointer-identity hazard: two functions that are byte-identical may still be non-mergeable if either has its address taken and the program relies on `&f != &g` (C/C++ guarantee that distinct functions have distinct addresses). Sound ICF therefore must exclude address-taken symbols (or prove the program never compares the addresses) — this is the soundness condition you'd formalize. None of this is in the evidence, but it is the cleanest "graph-bisimulation soundness theorem" candidate.

## (5) Confluence of sharded symbol resolution

No retrieved source addresses this. From general theory (uncited): symbol resolution can be modeled as repeated insertion into a map where each key's value is the join in a **selection semilattice** (e.g., strong defeats weak defeats common; ties broken by a total order on definition sites). If the per-symbol combination operator is associative, commutative, and idempotent (a true join-semilattice), then **sharded concurrent insertion is confluent**: the final symbol table is independent of insertion order/sharding, which is a standard CRDT-style result. The proof obligation is showing the real ELF resolution rules (weak/strong/common/COMDAT/version scripts) actually form a semilattice — they *mostly* do but COMDAT-group and version-script semantics introduce order sensitivities that may break commutativity. Verifying *that* is a concrete, bounded Coq target.
